# Sales, Proposals & Pricing

Quick, single-task prompts for winning the work — turning discovery notes into proposals, building packages, pricing with confidence, and handling the "why does it cost that" conversation.

*Prompts 31–40 of 50.*

> **Privacy first:** anonymize prospect/client details before pasting. These prompts produce drafts — your judgment sets the final scope and price.

---

### 1. Draft a cleanup proposal from notes

**When to use:** You scoped a cleanup/catch-up project and want a polished proposal from your rough notes.

```text
You are a bookkeeping firm owner writing a cleanup/catch-up project proposal for a prospect. Tone: confident, clear, value-led.

From my notes below, draft a proposal for a {{INDUSTRY}} client covering {{NUMBER OF MONTHS}} of catch-up.

Structure:
1. Brief summary of their current situation (in their words — show I understood it).
2. What's included (scope of the cleanup: reconciliations, categorization, adjusting entries, chart of accounts, deliverables).
3. What's NOT included (clear boundaries — e.g., tax filing, prior years beyond scope, ongoing monthly work).
4. Timeline / phases.
5. Investment: {{PRICE or "leave a placeholder [$X]"}} and payment terms.
6. Next step to get started.

Keep it skimmable with headers. Frame the price as an investment in clean books and peace of mind, not hours. Flag any item (prior-year adjustments, tax positions) that should go to their CPA. End with a clear CTA.

My notes:
{{PASTE DISCOVERY/SCOPE NOTES}}
```

### 2. Draft a monthly engagement proposal

**When to use:** A prospect wants ongoing monthly bookkeeping and you need a proposal for the recurring relationship.

```text
You are a bookkeeping firm owner writing a proposal for ongoing monthly bookkeeping. Tone: confident, professional, outcome-focused.

Details:
- Prospect: {{NAME/INDUSTRY}}
- Their situation/pain: {{NOTES}}
- Scope: {{e.g., monthly categorization, reconcile 2 accounts, monthly P&L + Balance Sheet, monthly check-in}}
- Add-ons if relevant: {{e.g., AP, AR, sales tax tracking, 1099s}}
- Monthly price: {{PRICE or "[$X/mo]"}}

Draft a proposal with: a short situation summary, what's included each month (clear bullets), what's excluded, communication/deliverable cadence (when they get reports), the monthly investment and terms, and a get-started CTA. Emphasize the outcome — accurate, current books and clarity to run the business — over the task list. Keep it professional and skimmable. Note that tax filing/advice is handled by their CPA, not included.
```

### 3. Build tiered service packages

**When to use:** You want a clean Good / Better / Best package structure to present instead of one custom quote.

```text
You are a bookkeeping firm owner designing tiered monthly service packages for {{TARGET CLIENT — e.g., small service businesses doing $X–$Y revenue}}.

Create three tiers (e.g., Essentials / Growth / Premium). For each, give: a one-line positioning statement, the ideal client for that tier, what's included (clear list), and a suggested monthly price range for the U.S. market (note these are starting ranges I'll adjust). Make each tier a clear step up — more accounts, more frequency, more advisory, more support — so the middle tier is the obvious "most popular" choice.

After the three tiers, add: 2–3 optional add-ons (e.g., catch-up cleanup, 1099 filing, AP/AR management) and a short note on how to position the tiers on a call so prospects self-select. Keep advisory items within bookkeeping scope; route tax filing/advice to a CPA.
```

### 4. Build a pricing estimate from hours

**When to use:** You've estimated the hours and want to turn that into a defensible flat price.

```text
You are a bookkeeping firm owner pricing an engagement. Help me convert an hours estimate into a flat fee.

Inputs:
- Engagement type: {{e.g., one-time cleanup / ongoing monthly}}
- Estimated hours: {{HOURS — give a range if you have one}}
- My target effective rate: {{HOURLY_RATE}}
- Complexity factors: {{e.g., messy books, multiple accounts, Stripe, payroll, sales tax}}
- Risk/uncertainty: {{LOW/MED/HIGH}}

Walk me through: (1) base price from hours × rate, (2) a recommended adjustment for complexity and uncertainty (with a buffer for scope creep), (3) a suggested flat-fee or price-range to quote, and (4) for ongoing work, a monthly figure. Explain your reasoning briefly so I can defend it. Remind me that flat/value pricing should reflect the outcome and my expertise, not just my hours — and to cap scope so unlimited work doesn't erode the rate.
```

### 5. Handle pricing objections

**When to use:** A prospect pushed back on price and you want confident, non-defensive responses ready.

```text
You are a sales coach for bookkeeping firm owners. A prospect raised a pricing objection and I want to respond with confidence, not discounts.

The objection: {{e.g., "That's more than I expected" / "My last bookkeeper charged half" / "Can you do it cheaper?" / "I could just use a cheaper software"}}
Context: {{their situation, what I proposed, the price}}

Give me 2–3 response options for this objection that: acknowledge the concern, reframe around value/outcomes/risk of cheap-or-DIY bookkeeping, and hold the price (or offer a scope adjustment instead of a discount). Keep them natural and conversational — phrasing I'd actually say on a call. Add one line on the underlying psychology of this objection and a "don't say this" warning of a common mistake. Don't be pushy or salesy.
```

### 6. Write a discovery-call script

**When to use:** You want a structured set of questions and flow for a sales/discovery call with a prospect.

```text
You are a bookkeeping firm owner. Write me a discovery-call script/framework for talking to prospects in the {{INDUSTRY}} space (or general if I leave it blank).

Structure the call: (1) warm open and agenda-setting, (2) discovery questions that uncover their current bookkeeping situation, pain, what's behind, how they're doing it now, and what good would look like, (3) questions that surface urgency and budget without being awkward, (4) a transition to presenting how I help, and (5) a clear next step / close.

For the questions section, group them and keep each short and open-ended. Add notes on what I'm listening for in their answers (red flags, fit signals, scope clues). Keep the whole thing usable as a one-page guide I can glance at during the call. Note that any tax/legal questions get flagged to a CPA/attorney, not answered live.
```

### 7. Draft a scope of work

**When to use:** You need a clear, boundary-setting scope of work to attach to a proposal or engagement letter.

```text
You are a bookkeeping firm owner drafting the scope of work for an engagement. Be precise — clear scope prevents scope creep and disputes.

Engagement details:
- Type: {{ongoing monthly / cleanup / hybrid}}
- Industry: {{INDUSTRY}}
- Services in scope: {{LIST}}
- Frequency/deliverables: {{e.g., monthly P&L + BS by the 15th}}
- Software: QuickBooks Online

Produce a scope of work with: Services Included (specific, itemized), Deliverables & Timing, Client Responsibilities (what they must provide and by when), Explicitly Out of Scope (e.g., tax preparation/advice, audit support, prior periods, payroll processing — unless I add them), Assumptions (e.g., transaction volume, number of accounts), and how additional/out-of-scope work is handled and billed. Use clear, plain professional language. This is a scope document, not legal advice — note that the engagement letter/legal terms should be reviewed by an attorney.
```

### 8. Plan a rate increase across your client base

**When to use:** You want to raise rates and need a strategy, not just one email.

```text
You are a pricing advisor for bookkeeping firm owners. Help me plan a rate increase across my existing clients.

My situation:
- Number of clients: {{NUMBER}}
- Current pricing: {{RANGE}}
- Why I'm raising: {{e.g., underpriced, scope grew, rising costs, first increase in years}}
- Target new pricing: {{RANGE or %}}

Give me a rollout plan: (1) how to segment clients (e.g., most underpriced first, highest-maintenance, longest-tenured), (2) how much to raise each segment and whether to phase it, (3) timing and notice period, (4) how to handle likely pushback or a client who threatens to leave, and (5) a short script/talking points for the conversation. Be realistic about which clients I might lose and why that can be okay. Keep me confident and professional throughout. Don't draft the full email here — just the strategy and key talking points.
```

### 9. Build a package-comparison table

**When to use:** You want a clean side-by-side comparison of your packages to put in a proposal or on your site.

```text
You are a bookkeeping firm owner. Turn my service tiers into a clean side-by-side comparison table a prospect can scan in 20 seconds.

I'll paste my tiers and what each includes (or tell you to use a standard 3-tier structure). 

Produce a comparison table: rows = features/services (reconciliations, # of accounts, reporting frequency, AP/AR, sales tax, 1099s, advisory calls, support response time, etc.), columns = each tier, cells = ✓ / number / "—". Put price at the top of each column. Mark the recommended/"most popular" tier. Below the table, write a one-line summary of who each tier is for. Keep it clean and honest — don't fabricate features I didn't list. 

My tiers:
{{PASTE TIERS or "use a standard Essentials/Growth/Premium structure"}}
```

### 10. Justify the ROI to a prospect

**When to use:** A prospect needs to see that good bookkeeping pays for itself before they'll commit.

```text
You are a bookkeeping firm owner making the value/ROI case to a prospect who's unsure bookkeeping is worth the cost. Tone: confident, concrete, never preachy.

Prospect context:
- Industry: {{INDUSTRY}}
- Their situation: {{e.g., DIY-ing it, behind, stressed at tax time, no idea if profitable}}
- What I'd charge: {{PRICE}}

Build the value case: (1) the real costs of their current approach (their time, missed deductions, late fees, scrambling at tax time, decisions made blind, errors) — quantify in plain terms where reasonable, (2) what they get back (hours, clarity, tax-ready books, fewer surprises, better decisions), (3) a simple framing that the fee is small relative to what clean books protect and unlock. Give me a few concrete, believable talking points I can use on a call — not hype. Be honest; don't promise specific tax savings (that's the CPA's lane) — flag it as something their tax pro confirms.
```
