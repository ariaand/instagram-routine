# Firm Operations & Marketing

Quick, single-task prompts for running and growing the firm — turning your know-how into SOPs and checklists, and your expertise into content that brings clients to you.

*Prompts 41–50 of 50.*

> **Privacy first:** keep client specifics out of marketing content unless you have permission and have anonymized it. Operational prompts are about your firm — your judgment finalizes every SOP.

---

### 1. Turn a described workflow into an SOP

**When to use:** You do a task the same way every time and want it documented as a standard operating procedure.

```text
You are a process documentation expert who writes SOPs for bookkeeping firms. Turn my description below into a clean, repeatable standard operating procedure.

The process:
{{DESCRIBE THE WORKFLOW IN YOUR OWN WORDS — what you do, in what order, in QBO and elsewhere}}

Produce an SOP with: Title, Purpose (why this exists), When to run it / trigger, Who's responsible, Tools/access needed, then numbered Steps (each step a single clear action; include where in QBO and what to click where relevant), Quality checks / "done when," and Common pitfalls to avoid. Use plain, imperative language ("Open the bank feed," "Match the deposit"). Make it detailed enough that a new team member could follow it without me. Flag any step that requires professional judgment or a CPA so it's not blindly automated.
```

### 2. Build a client offboarding checklist

**When to use:** A client is leaving and you want a complete, professional wind-down so nothing is missed.

```text
You are a bookkeeping firm owner. Build me a complete client offboarding checklist so I can wind down an engagement cleanly and professionally.

Context:
- Service type: {{ongoing monthly / cleanup / etc.}}
- Software: QuickBooks Online
- Reason: {{client leaving / mutual / we're parting ways}}

Produce a checklist organized in phases: (1) Finalize the books (complete the current/last period, reconcile, deliver final reports), (2) Financial close-out (final invoice, collect outstanding payment, settle any credits), (3) Access & data handoff (transfer or remove QBO access, return documents, revoke portal/credentials), (4) Communication (offboarding email, confirm end date, ask for a testimonial/referral if appropriate), (5) Internal cleanup (archive files securely, remove from recurring task lists, update my records). Format as checkboxes. Add a note on securely handling and retaining their data per best practice.
```

### 3. Write an Instagram post

**When to use:** You want a single educational/engaging Instagram caption for your firm.

```text
You are a content writer for a bookkeeping firm. Write an Instagram caption.

Details:
- My niche: {{NICHE — e.g., restaurants, creatives, trades}}
- Brand voice (3 words): {{e.g., approachable, sharp, no-nonsense}}
- Topic/angle: {{e.g., a common bookkeeping mistake, a QBO tip, a deadline, a myth}}
- Goal of the post: {{educate / build trust / soft CTA}}

Write a caption that: opens with a scroll-stopping first line (hook), delivers one genuinely useful idea in short, skimmable lines, and ends with a light call to action or question to drive comments. Plain language a small-business owner gets — no jargon. Keep it concise. Add 5–8 relevant hashtags at the end and one suggestion for the visual/image to pair with it. Keep claims honest (no guaranteed tax savings); if a tax point comes up, keep it general and "talk to your CPA."
```

### 4. Write a LinkedIn post

**When to use:** You want a credibility-building LinkedIn post that reads as a peer/expert, not an ad.

```text
You are a content writer helping a bookkeeping firm owner post on LinkedIn. Write a single post.

Details:
- My niche: {{NICHE}}
- Brand voice: {{3 WORDS}}
- Topic/angle: {{e.g., a lesson from a cleanup, a money mistake owners make, a behind-the-scenes of good bookkeeping}}
- Goal: {{authority / start a conversation / attract referrals}}

Write a LinkedIn post that opens with a strong first line (LinkedIn cuts off after ~2 lines, so earn the "see more"), tells a short story or makes a clear point with white space between short paragraphs, delivers real insight (not fluff), and ends with a question or soft CTA to spark comments. Professional but human — no corporate buzzwords, no hashtag spam (2–3 max). Keep it honest; route any tax specifics to "your CPA."
```

### 5. Outline an Instagram carousel

**When to use:** You want a multi-slide carousel that teaches something and ends with a CTA.

```text
You are a content strategist for a bookkeeping firm. Outline an Instagram (or LinkedIn) carousel, slide by slide.

Details:
- Niche: {{NICHE}}
- Brand voice: {{3 WORDS}}
- Topic: {{e.g., "5 bookkeeping mistakes that cost you at tax time", "how to know if your books are clean", "what a bookkeeper actually does"}}
- Number of slides: {{e.g., 7}}

For each slide give me: the on-slide headline/text (short — carousels are visual, so a line or two max) and a one-line note on the visual or layout. Slide 1 = a strong hook that makes people swipe. Middle slides = one idea each, building logically. Second-to-last = the payoff/summary. Last slide = a clear CTA (follow, save, DM, book a call). Below the outline, write a short caption to post with it and 5–8 hashtags. Keep advice accurate and jargon-free; keep tax points general.
```

### 6. Draft a monthly newsletter

**When to use:** You send (or want to start) a monthly email newsletter to your list and need a draft.

```text
You are a copywriter for a bookkeeping firm writing the monthly email newsletter. Audience: small-business owners on my list (clients and prospects). Voice: {{3 WORDS}}. Niche: {{NICHE or "general small business"}}.

This month's theme/topic: {{e.g., a tax deadline coming up, year-end prep, a money tip, a seasonal cash-flow reminder}}

Write a newsletter with: a subject line (give me 3 options), a warm short intro, one main useful section (a tip, reminder, or lesson they can act on — practical and specific), an optional quick "in case you missed it" or seasonal note, and a clear but low-pressure CTA (book a call / reply / refer a friend). Keep it skimmable with short paragraphs and maybe a header or two. Friendly and genuinely helpful, not salesy. Keep tax content general and point readers to their CPA for specifics. End with a sign-off placeholder [Your name / Firm].
```

### 7. Write a referral request

**When to use:** You want to ask happy clients or contacts for referrals without it feeling awkward.

```text
You are helping a bookkeeping firm owner ask for referrals gracefully. Write a referral request.

Context:
- Who I'm asking: {{e.g., a happy long-term client / past clients / my network}}
- Channel: {{email / DM / end of a call}}
- Ideal referral: {{describe my best-fit client — industry, size, situation}}
- Any incentive: {{e.g., none / referral thank-you / discount — or leave blank}}

Write a short, warm message that: leads with genuine appreciation (or a recent win if a client), makes the ask clearly but without pressure, describes exactly who's a great fit for me so they know who to think of, and makes it easy to act (forward this, intro me, share my link). Keep it natural and human — not a form letter. Under ~130 words. Give me one version for a current client and one for a general network contact. End with [Your name].
```

### 8. Brainstorm a lead magnet

**When to use:** You want a free resource idea that attracts your ideal client and grows your email list.

```text
You are a marketing strategist for bookkeeping firms. Help me come up with a lead magnet (free resource) that attracts my ideal client.

My niche: {{NICHE}}
My ideal client's biggest pain: {{e.g., behind on books, stressed at tax time, no idea if profitable, DIY overwhelm}}
What I want it to lead to: {{e.g., discovery calls, newsletter signups, monthly clients}}

Give me 5 lead-magnet ideas (checklists, templates, mini-guides, quizzes, calculators, etc.) tailored to this niche and pain. For each: a working title, what's in it (briefly), why this niche would actually want it, and how it sets up my paid offer. Then recommend the single best one to build first and why, plus a 3-step plan to create it fast. Keep the resource genuinely useful and accurate; keep any tax content general with a "consult your CPA" note.
```

### 9. Write a bookkeeper job description

**When to use:** You're hiring and need a clear, attractive job description for a bookkeeper or assistant.

```text
You are an HR/recruiting writer helping a bookkeeping firm owner hire. Write a job description.

Details:
- Role: {{e.g., staff bookkeeper / bookkeeping assistant / senior bookkeeper}}
- Employment type: {{full-time / part-time / contract}}, {{remote / hybrid / on-site}}
- Core duties: {{e.g., categorization, reconciliations in QBO, month-end close support, client comms}}
- Must-have skills: {{e.g., QBO proficiency, X years experience, attention to detail}}
- Nice-to-have: {{e.g., specific industries, payroll, sales tax}}
- About my firm: {{a sentence or two — niche, size, culture}}

Write a job description with: an engaging intro about the firm and role, Responsibilities, Required qualifications, Preferred qualifications, What we offer / why work here, and how to apply. Make it attractive to a quality candidate (firms compete for good bookkeepers) while being clear about expectations. Keep it professional and specific — no fluff. Leave [salary/range] and [application link] as placeholders.
```

### 10. Standardize a messy process into a checklist

**When to use:** You do something inconsistently and want it boiled down to a simple, repeatable checklist.

```text
You are a process expert for bookkeeping firms. Take my loose description of a recurring task and turn it into a tight, repeatable checklist I can run every time without thinking.

The task and how I currently do it (messy is fine):
{{DESCRIBE — e.g., my weekly transaction-categorization routine, my new-client setup, my end-of-week wrap-up}}

Produce: a clear checklist title, the trigger (when to run it / how often), and an ordered list of checkbox steps — each a single, concrete action in plain imperative language. Remove ambiguity: if I described something vaguely, make it specific or add a [confirm: ...] placeholder. Put the steps in the most efficient order. Add a short "done when" line at the bottom so I know it's complete, and a note of 1–2 things people most often forget on this kind of task. Keep it lean — a checklist, not an essay.
```
